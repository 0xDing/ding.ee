To meet in words is still a kind of meeting. I’m Li (Boris) Ding.

I like to think of myself as a mongrel in the school of Hofstadter, Adler, and Wang Xiaobo—and a Humean agnostic besides.

The things I’ve done over the past few years have been somewhat eclectic: I’ve sketched interaction flows, done user research and motion design, written frontend code and iOS apps, and hand-rolled microservice frameworks and data systems; I’ve done white-hat security work and found vulnerabilities; in AI, I’ve built agent orchestration frameworks, trained base LLMs and text-to-image models from scratch, and contributed to some open-source work in multimodality and SFT. Startups, product, systems, AI, quant—I’ve had a hand in all of them.

At China Renaissance, I helped incubate Zhulu X, an internet-native FA platform. I also helped Sequoia China build its data science team from the ground up and developed a quantitative approach to primary-market investing there. Later, I went out to build a SaaS product of my own: something halfway between software and documents, with a dash of low-code, meant to dissolve the always-suspect boundary between the two. In time, that made me one of the glorious early casualties.

Strictly speaking, my professional identity has never been especially stable. One thing has remained stable, though: the desire to make concrete things, and to change the world, however slightly. I’ve always valued breadth over depth, and I take a certain pride in that.

These days, I’m mainly occupied by two things: quantitative finance and Agentic RL.

What draws me to the former is not merely the worldly business of making money. Markets are interesting because they are not quiet places, nor especially reasonable ones. Many narratives in finance like to assume that people are rational, markets tend toward equilibrium, and information glides gracefully into price the way water settles into a pond. I’ve never had much affection for that picture. I’d rather look at markets through the lens of complexity economics and behavioral economics: as crowded, noisy, feedback-laden adaptive systems, where people rush in carrying bias, emotion, partial information, and the burden of their own circumstances—altering one another, and prices along with it. From that angle, quant becomes far more interesting. It is not merely the prediction of a string of numbers, but an attempt to trace how narratives spread, how conviction forms, how biases accumulate, and how all of that eventually takes shape in price.

The latter speaks to another question that has long held my attention: what would it mean for an intelligent agent to actually work? I’ve never been especially interested in toy agents that stop at RAG plus function calling. What I care about are the harder, more fundamental things: long-term memory, world models, self-calibration, task decomposition, and continual learning. To me, Agentic RL is less a fashionable label than a plain obsession: I want to see whether intelligent systems can move a little farther—from speaking, to doing, and from doing to becoming a little more real with each iteration.

This blog will be about products, systems, AI, quant, and aesthetics, but also about the longer-running questions beneath them: judgment, method, and creation. The topics may not always be stable, but the underlying interests are. It is, in the end, all part of the same effort: to understand complex things, to build new ones, and to keep revising oneself in the presence of change.

At twenty-one, my birthday wish was to refactor the real world.
At thirty-two, it is to admit more bias into the way I love, the way I live, the works I make, and the way I welcome random perturbations.
