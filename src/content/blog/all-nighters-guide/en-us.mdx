## Unless Absolutely Necessary, Don't Stay Up Late
Research has found that sleep deprivation leads to metabolic disruption[^1], and there is a well-established link between insufficient sleep and risk factors for numerous metabolic diseases, including weight gain, diabetes, obesity, and cardiovascular disease. Moreover, the damage that sleep deprivation inflicts on the brain **cannot be repaired by catching up on sleep**[^2] (mice subjected to prolonged sleep deprivation lost 25% of the neurons in certain brain regions. That said, **catching up is always better than not catching up at all**).

It is generally accepted that adults need at least 6 to 8 hours of sleep per day. However, research[^3] suggests that **for most people, six hours is simply not enough**. In a 14-day experiment, subjects who slept eight hours showed virtually no signs of attention lapses or cognitive decline. Meanwhile, subjects in the four- and six-hour groups saw their PVT (Psychomotor Vigilance Task) scores deteriorate almost every day, with the four-hour group faring the worst. By day six, 25% of the six-hour group had fallen asleep at their computers. By the end of the study, their attention lapses were five times more frequent than on day one.

**Therefore, maintaining eight hours of sleep as a baseline is essential. Occasionally settling for six hours during busy periods is acceptable. However, sleeping less than six hours should be avoided at all costs — unless a truly exceptional emergency arises.**

#### Prevent Insomnia
* **Don't count sheep** — research[^4] shows it actually makes you more alert.
* **Take a hot bath about half an hour before bed.** Fluctuations in body temperature signal to your brain when to feel drowsy and when to feel alert. When body temperature rises, you tend to feel awake and your brainwave frequency is typically higher. When it drops, you tend to feel sleepy and sluggish. After a hot bath, your body temperature dips slightly[^5], nudging you toward sleep. Regular evening physical activity and aerobic exercise also play an important role in temperature regulation. **Exercise can rapidly raise your body temperature.**
* **Sleeping in on weekends is detrimental to your sleep cycle.** Those extra hours reduce your exposure to sunlight, which slows down your body temperature's natural rise and fall. The result? Difficulty falling asleep on Sunday night — the phenomenon commonly known as "Sunday night insomnia." Try to keep your weekend sleep schedule consistent with weekdays so that your body temperature rhythm stays on track. Once your body has established a routine, altering your sleep habits disrupts your temperature cycle, making it harder to achieve deep sleep.
* **Sleep-onset insomnia (difficulty falling asleep):**
  * **Slow down your racing mind.** Many people lie in bed with thoughts racing because they have excess mental energy left over from the evening. You need to calm your mind. Here's how: slowly repeat the words that surface in your thoughts. If you have a strong visual imagination, write those words out in your mind's eye, as if using pen and paper. Repeat this process, going slower each time. Your brain may resist at first, and a storm of thoughts may return. Don't worry — just keep at it, and you'll find yourself relaxing and drifting off before long.
  * **Sleep restriction.** If you've been lying in bed for more than 30 minutes without falling asleep, get up and wait until drowsiness hits before returning to bed.
  * **Take melatonin.** As a last resort, taking melatonin **when necessary** is a reasonable option. It can significantly improve your sleep quality. However, you should never take prescription sleeping pills — unless explicitly directed by a doctor.
* **Sleep-maintenance insomnia (poor sleep quality, waking up in the middle of the night):**
   * **Make sure your curtains block out all light.** Research shows that even light exposure on the skin alone can affect melatonin levels in the body.
   * Make sure your sleeping position and room temperature are within your comfort zone. Generally speaking, **a cool environment is more conducive to sleep**.
   * If you experience persistent sleep-maintenance issues that affect your daily life, **consult a licensed mental health professional to rule out the possibility of depression or anxiety disorders**.

## When Circumstances Force Your Hand: Recovery After an All-Nighter
* **Nap for 20 minutes, or split it into two 10-minute naps.** While napping can't bring back the neurons that died during last night's sleep deprivation, when you're utterly exhausted, you'll spend the entire day in a semi-conscious haze — drifting off, waking up, drifting off again. A brief nap effectively reduces these episodes of "microsleep."
* **Sip coffee slowly.** There is abundant evidence that caffeine improves alertness, and its "eye-opening effects" work best when consumed in small, steady sips — about 50 grams of coffee (roughly 50 ml) or 100 grams of tea per hour yields the best results. But stop drinking coffee at least six hours before bedtime.
* **Replenish vitamins and electrolytes.** A multivitamin tablet and a bottle of an electrolyte drink (such as Pocari Sweat) will do the job nicely.
* **Eat a sensible breakfast.** You need some calories to keep your mind sharp, but avoid foods with a high glycemic index — they'll make you drowsy. Skip the chocolate, pastries, and candy. Sandwiches, eggs, and milk are much better choices.
