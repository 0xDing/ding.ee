## Grand Ambitions from Humble Beginnings

> "By 'augmenting human intellect' we mean increasing the capability of a man to approach a complex problem situation, to gain comprehension to suit his particular needs, and to derive solutions to problems. Increased capability in this respect is taken to mean a mixture of the following: more-rapid comprehension, better comprehension, the possibility of gaining a useful degree of comprehension in a situation that previously was too complex, speedier solutions, better solutions, and the possibility of finding solutions to problems that before seemed insoluble. And by 'complex situations' we include the professional problems of diplomats, executives, social scientists, life scientists, physical scientists, attorneys, designers — whether the problems last twenty minutes or twenty years. We do not speak of isolated clever tricks that help in particular situations. We refer to a way of life in an integrated domain where hunches, cut-and-try, intangibles, and the human 'feel for a situation' usefully co-exist with powerful concepts, streamlined terminology and notation, sophisticated methods, and high-powered electronic aids.
> The population is growing at a rate that is matched by the growth in gross human product, but the problems it faces are growing still faster, and solutions must be found — the urgency of which increases with the scale and global reach of these challenges. Augmenting human intellect, then, if pursued through reasonable methods and with even plausible benefits, warrants thorough investigation by an open society.
> Excerpted from *Augmenting Human Intellect: A Conceptual Framework* by Douglas C. Engelbart

Hey, welcome aboard!

Brickdoc is a software company whose mission is to "augment human intellect" by building productivity tools. More specifically, Brickdoc is a cloud-native Office suite for `citizen developers`. By integrating online documents, RPA, and a cloud event bus, it blurs the line between documents and software — empowering users to automate parts of their work without writing a single line of code. **If everyone in the world could write a simple Python script, the overall productivity of society would increase exponentially.**

The way we do things is rather unique. It will be the best experience of your career, though it may take a little while to get used to. This document was written by people who were once new here too, and their goal is to ease the bumps during your first few months.

## The Brickdoc Way

### Never Repeat Yourself

> Some people think that spending a lot of time building automation tools is less efficient than just doing things manually — for instance, spending five hours writing an automation script when repeating the task 200 times would only take three hours. People who think this way fundamentally misunderstand engineering.
> On the one hand, the tool can be shared and reused so that more people benefit from it. The five hours I spend building it today means only one hour of tweaking next time to apply it elsewhere. This is thinking about the future, not just the cost right now. More importantly, this is a culture — a culture of improving efficiency — and it encourages and inspires more of the same.
> Excerpted from [What Is Engineering Culture?](http://coolshell.cn/articles/17497.html) by Chen Hao

We believe the essence of engineering culture is not unlimited snacks or cool T-shirts (though we do offer both XD). It is about **finding root causes and solving problems once and for all**. In practice, this means:

- **Ruthlessly pursue automation.** Your time **must** be spent on the most creative work. For us, internal productivity tools are just as important as user-facing products.
- **Exercise restraint.** Complex systems breed entropy. We favor simpler solutions. This also means that **if an existing SaaS product or an open-source project can meet the need, it should be the first choice**.
- **Continuously improve.** We take retrospectives extremely seriously and seek **root-cause solutions** to every issue we find.

### Forget Your Title

Hierarchical org structures are great for maintaining predictability and repeatability. They simplify processes and make it easier to manage large numbers of people from the top down — which is exactly why the military values them so highly.

But if we go to enormous lengths to find the best people, only to have them follow their job descriptions to the letter, we would be missing the forest for the trees. We need innovators, so we build them a stage to shine on. **Please forget your title. Brickdoc belongs to everyone who works here, and everyone at Brickdoc represents Brickdoc.** Everyone is equal, and **you have the authority to do anything you believe is valuable for Brickdoc.**

**This does not mean we have no management structure, nor that you never "report" to anyone.** We have founders and a board of directors. But regardless, you help steer this company — toward opportunities and away from risks. We will never stop you from independently exploring user needs and working to meet them in your own way.

Oh, and to make this possible, **we share as much information as we can with everyone (and sometimes even the public)**. Information silos easily breed misunderstandings and misaligned efforts. Information equality spans everything from strategy, direction, and goals down to documentation, code, and knowledge sharing. It also applies to voicing opinions — everyone has the opportunity to share their ideas and suggestions, which sparks more creative thinking and healthy debate. We also strive to **minimize internal approval processes** — because approvals usually imply three things: (1) incomplete trust in people, (2) cumbersome processes, and (3) mental constraints. These are the natural enemies of innovation and imagination.

**But always remember: "With great power comes great responsibility, and trust is often conditional."** This is why we only hire the best. When interviewing candidates, beyond evaluating their teamwork skills and domain expertise, always consider whether they have the ability to run this company themselves — because that is what they will actually be doing.

### Everyone Is a Polymath

> There is a payoff for being a heretic — not just in science, but anywhere there is competition. If you can see what others cannot or dare not see, you have a huge advantage. Train yourself to think the unthinkable, and the benefit you gain will far exceed the ideas themselves. Your mind is an underground — never blurt out everything that happens down there. The first rule of Fight Club is: you do not talk about Fight Club. Those who can discuss "heresy" without losing their temper are exactly the friends you should seek out.
> If you are part of the current yourself, how can you see which way it flows? You can only keep questioning. Ask yourself: what can I not say? And why?
> Excerpted from [*Hackers and Painters*](https://book.douban.com/annotation/17944355/) by Paul Graham

TRIZ theory holds that the hallmark of product innovation is resolving or removing design conflicts to produce new, competitive solutions. Designers who continuously discover and resolve conflicts are the driving force behind product evolution.

**To a great extent, cross-disciplinary knowledge, applied through analogy, can achieve 1 + 1 > 2 efficiency and powerfully resolve design conflicts.** The word "design" can be traced to the Latin *de* (to make) and *signare* (to mark) — that is, to create something and give it symbolic meaning that sets it apart. Whether we are talking about the human mind in the physical world or system architecture in cyberspace, the act of symbolization and abstraction is a vital way to deconstruct "entropy" in complex systems. The essence of architecture is finding the right entry point for just the right level of abstraction so that complexity can be effectively managed.

We often draw fresh architectural insights and inspiration from "design-oriented" fields such as architecture, law, and economics. The well-known concept of "design patterns," for instance, originally came from the field of architecture. **We encourage team members to learn things outside their professional domain, including but not limited to general knowledge and social sciences. We will do our best to support and facilitate this kind of learning.**

### Speak Plainly

If you wanted to haggle over vegetables at the market, saying "Can you lower the price? Otherwise I'll check the next stall" is obviously more effective than saying "Your pricing is at the upper end of our acceptable range. The procurement approval won't go through. Let's realign. If the quote still doesn't meet expectations, we may need to initiate a new round of RFQs, which could impact your conversion funnel and monetization roadmap."

We believe the same logic applies at work.

#### Hiring Strategy

Nothing in the world is more important than hiring — **hiring is truly the most important thing in the universe. Finding talent and maintaining an extraordinarily high bar ranks above everything else.** So when you are in charge of recruiting, conducting an interview, or improving hiring materials, whatever else you have on your plate is secondary. Drop it.

"How to hire" is a thorny question that is hard to pin down, because we are constantly learning from the process. When evaluating candidates, we also ask ourselves:

* Would I want this person to be my boss?
* Would I learn a lot from them?
* What if this person were competing against us?

In general, we place tremendous value on collaborative talent. They must possess the qualities essential to a highly collaborative environment: the ability to analyze problems quickly and communicate with others, along with innovativeness, lateral thinking, creativity, articulate communication, and quick responsiveness. These qualities matter far more than deep expertise in a single narrow domain. That is why we typically say no to "specialists" who excel at only one thing.

**We want people who are better than us.** Left unchecked, people tend to hire those less capable than themselves. The three questions above serve as a reminder not to settle for someone who is useful but not as good as we are. We should bring in people more capable than ourselves. Sometimes, hiring less capable people might seem like it would get a lot of tedious work done more smoothly. In the short term, hiring someone "good enough" might look better than hiring no one at all. But that is a huge mistake. **We can hire contractors or outsource to get through tough periods, but we must never lower our hiring bar.**

## Measuring Contributions

### Performance Measurement

> When no one is there to show you the way, press on without fear.

We use two tools for performance measurement: OKRs and 360-degree reviews.

#### OKRs

OKR is a well-established goal management methodology. We operate on quarterly cycles — in late December, March, June, and September, we complete individual and team OKR setting and evaluate the previous quarter's delivery.

A quarterly OKR consists of several Objectives, each composed of several Key Results. KRs should be measurable and include both a "Meets Expectations" and an "Exceeds Expectations" tier.

For example, here is a typical set of individual quarterly OKRs:
"""
- Objective 1: Deliver iteration milestones on time
  - KR 1: Delayed requirements \<= 2
  - KR 2: Completed story points \>= 200
  - KR 3: [Exceeds Expectations] Zero delayed requirements
  - KR 4: [Exceeds Expectations] Completed story points \>= 250
- Objective 2: Address legacy technical debt in the Brickdoc project
  - KR 1: Complete front-end/back-end code separation
  - KR 2: Cyclomatic complexity warnings above 10 \<= 10
  - KR 3: Resolve all linter-flagged code duplication issues
  - KR 4: [Exceeds Expectations] Brickdoc project unit test coverage \>= 80%
- Objective 3: App crash rate below 2% in the last release of the quarter
  - KR 1: Unresolved Sentry issues \< 15
  - KR 2: Improve Sentry SDK for accurate stack trace reporting; issues without stack traces \< 5
  - KR 3: [Exceeds Expectations] Implement automatic container crash recovery
- Objective 4: Build personal and team technical influence
  - KR 1: Complete one internal tech-sharing presentation at Brickdoc
  - KR 2: Launch a team blog and publish one technical article
  - KR 3: [Exceeds Expectations] Deliver one public conference presentation under the company banner

As the end of a quarter approaches (late December, March, June, or September), we follow this process for OKR evaluation and planning:
1. All team members complete self-assessments and peer reviews for the previous quarter's OKRs
2. Department leads evaluate each member's OKR completion and conduct one-on-one discussions
3. Department leads draft next-quarter team OKRs aligned with company goals
4. Team members draft their individual OKRs aligned with team goals; these take effect once confirmed by the department lead and are made visible to everyone
"""

**OKRs are a goal management tool only. There is no top-down scoring, and they do not directly determine performance ratings.**

#### 360-Degree Reviews

We conduct 360-degree reviews every six months, because all of us need feedback on our performance to improve and ensure we are not regressing. In late June and late December, after OKR evaluations are complete, the three colleagues you work most closely with and your reporting manager will be asked to participate in a brief survey. All questions are open-ended rather than numerical (e.g., a 1-10 scale). Although this format requires more thought and time, it produces far more valuable feedback.

**The results are aggregated, organized, anonymized, and then delivered to you. Anonymous feedback certainly has its pros and cons, but we believe it is currently the best way to provide the most useful information to everyone.** That said, if you want someone to know your thoughts sooner, there is no need to hold them in until the annual review. In fact, frequent communication works much better because it is free from the formality of an official review.

Department leads will combine survey results with the past two quarters' OKR delivery to assign your semi-annual performance rating. After the evaluation, they will also hold a one-on-one meeting with you to discuss any potential issues.

The five performance tiers are defined as follows:
* Far Exceeded — You completed projects of significant business value with outstanding results that exceeded expectations, earning applause from the entire team. This is your moment of glory.
* Exceeded — You achieved results above expectations (typically reflected in OKRs), earning recognition from most colleagues.
* Achieved — You delivered results that met expectations.
* Partially Met — There is substantial room for improvement in your work over the past six months.
* Did Not Meet — Your work did not meet the team's expectations.

**360-degree reviews directly determine performance ratings, which in turn affect bonuses, salary adjustments, and promotions.** To help colleagues who receive a Partially Met or Did Not Meet rating, we will develop a Performance Improvement Plan (PIP) to provide support and coaching. A PIP must last at least one month, with the exact duration determined on a case-by-case basis. If improvement remains insufficient after a PIP has been carried out, a role change may be considered, with compensation, job responsibilities, work location, and capability requirements adjusted accordingly.

**But rest easy — no one at Brickdoc has ever been fired for making a mistake.** We believe that would make no sense. Giving employees the freedom to fail is one of the defining traits of Brickdoc. If employees were punished for mistakes, we could never expect much from them. Even costly mistakes — even those that cause significant losses — are treated as learning opportunities. Because every mistake can be corrected.

**Screwing up is a great learning opportunity. You may discover that many of your assumptions were wrong, or that your worldview needed a small adjustment.** If you refine your worldview and keep moving forward with a better perspective, you are back on track. Find ways to test things you have already taken for granted. Dare to try. Dare to collect more data. Screwing up also helps you improve your ability to anticipate and prevent bad outcomes. Ask yourself: "What if I'm right?" Ask yourself: "What if I'm wrong?" Then ask: "What's happening now?" If something completely unexpected occurs, try to figure out why.

**There are many paths to ultimate failure. Repeating the same mistake is one.** Ignoring what customers or colleagues tell you — before or after a failure — is another. Never disregard evidence, especially when it shows you are wrong.

### Base Salary

**We ensure that base compensation for all roles is slightly above the average for equivalent positions and levels at top-tier Chinese tech companies.** Specifically, we conduct a market compensation survey at year-end, and the resulting raises take effect in January's payroll. Rest assured, this adjustment is one-directional — we will never reduce your salary because market averages have dropped.

For colleagues at the same level in the same role, base salaries are identical.

**If you feel your compensation does not match your contributions, please speak up candidly. Rest assured — at Brickdoc, you will be surprised at how relaxed and straightforward these conversations are. If someone silently stews over their salary and cannot perform at their best, it is an unnecessary loss for both them and Brickdoc.**

### Bonuses

### Intellectual Property and Confidentiality

We follow the Balanced Employee Intellectual Property Agreement (BEIPA) proposed by the GitHub team. This means we will not attempt to claim ownership of intellectual property you create in your personal time. Everything you create outside of work belongs entirely to you.

However, intellectual property created in the following contexts belongs to Brickdoc:
- Work-for-hire created based on explicit job requirements
- Competitive products developed during your employment that relate to our existing or in-development products or services
- Internal productivity tools
- Products developed or improved using our intellectual property or brand endorsement

Furthermore, you are obligated to maintain the confidentiality of our or our clients' trade secrets, data, or other information specified in the Confidentiality Agreement that you encounter in the course of your work, and to make every effort to prevent any form of information leakage. **You should not discuss such matters with family members, in business or social circles, or in places where you might be overheard — such as taxis, public transit, conferences, elevators, or restaurants. You must also ensure such information is not disclosed on any online forum, including message boards, blogs, or chat rooms.**
