Cast your mind back to 1980. The IBM System/370 mainframe, occupying nearly 40 square meters of floor space, stood as the unchallenged marvel of its era — yet the CPU clock speed it could muster was less than one hundredth of an iPhone 11's. A 25-year-old Steve Jobs, speaking publicly, coined the phrase "a bicycle for the mind" as a metaphor for the future of personal computing. But looking back from 39 years on, what we actually ended up with is more like a train for the mind — unlike a bicycle, a train can only travel along pre-laid tracks, following a predetermined timetable.

In 1987, Apple released a remarkable piece of software called HyperCard — and no one, Apple included, could truly articulate what kind of software it was. Some saw it as a lightweight database and hypertext-enabled note-taking tool. Others considered it a multimedia document creation tool in the vein of Flash or PowerPoint. Still others believed it was a software development tool that even "muggle users" could pick up with ease. Its origin story was equally legendary: HyperCard was born from a vision that came to Bill Atkinson — one of Apple's earliest guiding spirits — during a dream induced by a psychedelic substance.

![HyperCard's interface](../../../assets/blogs/hypercard/640.png)

Let life be beautiful like summer flowers — and so it was. The Petronas Twin Towers in Kuala Lumpur, then the world's tallest building, used HyperCard to control their lighting systems. French automotive giant Renault built an inventory management system with it. Ward Cunningham, inspired by HyperCard, created the first wiki site in human history. JavaScript itself began as little more than a simple imitation of HyperCard and HyperTalk for the web environment. Even Sir Tim Berners-Lee and his colleague Robert Cailliau drew on HyperCard's inspiration to create the world's first web browser, opening the door to the age of the Web. People used it to manage companies, design presentations, create interactive text adventures akin to *The Invisible Guardian*, and even to build rather more risqué applications.

![Programs developed with HyperCard](../../../assets/blogs/hypercard/2.png)

And death, like autumn leaves, came with quiet grace. When Jobs returned to Apple in 1997 and streamlined the product line, he axed most projects — and HyperCard was, unfortunately, among them. (One persistent rumor holds that Jobs killed HyperCard because Apple's former CEO Gil Amelio was a devoted fan of it.) It now lies buried in the dusty archives of ancient tech history, little known to the world at large, with no true successor ever managing to offer a fully comparable set of capabilities.

![HyperCard's card interface](../../../assets/blogs/hypercard/card.png)

True to its name, the Card is the most central concept in HyperCard. In HyperCard's world, each independent file is called a Stack. Each Stack is composed of a number of Cards — much as each PowerPoint file is composed of a number of slides. The key difference is that most slides in a PowerPoint are organized linearly, one after another, whereas the cards within a Stack are often linked together in a non-linear fashion reminiscent of hyperlinks.

HyperCard also featured a form system similar to tools like JotForm or Typeform, allowing users to design forms through drag-and-drop. But unlike electronic forms in a PDF, HyperCard's data layer was actually closer to single-user relational databases like Access or SQLite — all changes were persisted in real time within the Stack file.

![HyperCard's scripting language](../../../assets/blogs/hypercard/code.png)

As HyperCard's scripting language, HyperTalk can in many ways be regarded as the most astonishing piece of its design. HyperTalk was an event-driven scripting language with syntax remarkably close to natural language. When a user triggered an action in the GUI — clicking a button or modifying a field's value, for instance — the HyperCard Runtime would translate these actions into events, and HyperTalk scripts would listen for those events to execute the corresponding responses. To a great extent, this was the earliest wellspring of inspiration for the modern HTML DOM event model.

Unlike today's various low-code development platforms or RPA tools, HyperCard's greatest achievement was this: it was a "PowerPoint" that could be used to build programs, not a dumbed-down "IDE." Users could get started effortlessly and go on to create genuinely valuable productivity tools, all without needing to understand a single detail of the operating system. Before and after HyperCard, programming has more or less remained the exclusive domain of professional programmers. As Wikipedia's commentary puts it: "HyperCard represented the pinnacle of Apple's efforts to produce a bicycle — a tool with which users could, with modest effort, achieve results far exceeding their expectations. The essence of open-source programming is to make programming so accessible that anyone can venture into the world of software creation."
